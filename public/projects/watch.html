another application of GTFS data: BUS DATA

the problem

I can’t get to class without crossing the ION tracks — the ION being the Region of Waterloo’s LRT, with trains passing campus about every five minutes. This frequency, while great for getting away from campus, seems to make it so I am stopped by a train every time I want to cross the tracks. If only I had a quick way to see in advance when the crossing gates would come down....

the solution

Grand River Transit, the agency behind the ION, has GTFS data available to anyone who wants it. I had already worked with GTFS data to make BUS DATA, which you should check out later. I thought, if I can determine when the trains will arrive at the station, I can extrapolate when the adjacent grade crossing will activate. Some constant offset for both northbound and southbound trains should do the trick.

the execution

The main goal was to make something that was super quick to access, so that I could find out as I was walking up to the crossing whether I should slow down or start running.

To ensure I knew how to work with GRT’s data, I added a command to my Discord bot that would give ION arrival times and use them to estimate the grade crossing activation time. It worked well: I could pull out my phone, send the command, and get all the information I needed. But what’s faster than typing something into a phone? A flick of the wrist.

I happen to have a Google Pixel Watch, so with the goal of making the data as easy to access as possible, I entered the world of custom watch faces, Wear OS complications, and everything that entails. I started by making a custom watch face in Watch Face Studio, the one in the picture at the top of this page. I would love it even if it didn’t do something unique, I’m glad I was able to use nice colours and a font of my choice.



It shows the following information.

  • The date, formatted the way I prefer
  • Battery level, heart rate, and step count
  • The time, of course, in twenty-four-hour format
  • The day’s low, current, and high temperature
  • ION_details, which in the watch face designer is just a placeholder


The watch face was quite easy to make, but the complication that was to replace ION_details was not. Android apps, and by extension Wear OS complications, are written in Kotlin, a language I had never used before. I had Gemini, part of Android Studio, translate my original Discord bot JavaScript into Kotlin. Then I had to fight with Gemini to get all the dependencies properly imported and functioning. Not fun, but it worked out in the end.

Two times are shown at the bottom of the watch face, as you can see in the picture at the top of this page. The first time is when the data was fetched, and the second is the estimated grade crossing activation time. Only minutes and seconds are shown to save space. It’s useful to know when the calculation was performed so you know if it’s at all accurate.

the results

It works really well! Except...much of the time. Turns out, to AGGRESSIVELY save battery life, the frequency of complication updates are dictated by the watch. So the time is often for a train that passed way earlier. I tried my hardest to force it to update frequently, but to no avail. It works well enough though. I’m super happy with it.
